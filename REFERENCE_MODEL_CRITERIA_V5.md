# Reference Model Generation Criteria (V5)

## General Rules
- References to figures are to be found in the source "Blessing_Chakrabarti_2009_DRM, a Design Research Methodology".
- Restrictions not explicitly mentioned apply as described in the source “Blessing_Chakrabarti_2009_DRM, a Design Research Methodology”. Neither the explicitly stated rules nor the implicit rules derived from the source “Blessing_Chakrabarti_2009_DRM, a Design Research Methodology” may be violated!
- No factors (whether influencing factors, success factors, etc.) may be made up. Only the factors present in the main model may be used. This rule must not be broken at any time. The "generated by AI" criteria described in section "0.1 Information" are excluded.
- If, for some inexplicable reason, you do deviate from the rules or break them (whether consciously or unconsciously), make this transparently visible to the user at all times and explain why you did not adhere to the rules.

# Reference Model Criteria (V5)

### 0. Overview
Reference Models represent the existing situation for a specific problem scope.

##### 0.1 Information
Key Factors are those influencing factors that seem to be the most useful factors to address in order to improve an existing situation. These are considered the core factors or the root causes. The Key Factors are addressed directly by the support.
Statements that are found in the literature cannot simply be reversed: if e.g. high costs lead to reduced sales, this does not imply that low costs lead to high sales. The latter would be an assumption. It is important to base the Reference Model on the original statements, even if this implies a noncontinuous line of argumentation. The Reference Model represents the current understanding as-is. Assumptions can be added that differ from the original statements, as long as they are labelled as such. The Impact Model, in describing the desired situation, provides the freedom to change statements, but here too, these have to be marked as assumptions.

Success Criteria and Measurable Success Criteria
Success Criteria relate to the ultimate goal to which the research project or program intends to contribute. These criteria usually reveal the purpose of the research and the eventual, expected contribution to practice.
In the Reference and Impact Models, Success Criteria relate to the Success Factors. These are the factors at ‘the top’ of the network, i.e., at the end of the cause–effect chains that provide the justification of the research. The desired values of the Success Factors are taken as Success Criteria.

In addition to the observed problem, the user must enter an anticipated “Success Criteria” and “Measurable Success Criteria” because “… criteria are needed to be able to judge the outcome of the research against the research goals.” 
If the user is unable to explicitly name one or both of these, or actively refuses to provide the input, then one is anticipated and derived based on the "observed problem." However, this must then be appropriately marked as a criterion "generated by AI".
Following the proposal ("generated by AI"), the user must be given the option to either accept the criterion and proceed with the subsequent steps or to modify it. This initial proposal ("generated by AI") provides the user with a general frame of reference and allows them to evaluate whether the anticipated goal has been correctly selected from their perspective.
Only after confirming or adjusting the criterion can either an RM based on the proposal (“generated by AI”) be finalized and a corresponding IM subsequently generated, or—following adjustment—the RM be iterated with the user until it is complete from their perspective, allowing the corresponding IM to be derived.
If a research goal refers to several factors, several criteria have to be formulated.

For measuring success, we need Measurable Success Criteria. Measurable Success Criteria, i.e., criteria that are linked to the chosen Success Criteria and can be applied to judge the outcomes of the research, given the resources available within the project or program.
The factors whose desired values are taken as Measurable Success Criteria are the Measurable Success Factors. Measurable Success Factor can be all influencing factors with value “1” for “measurability”. If, within the specified parameters (number of permissible influencing factors for RM), no influencing factor with a measurability value of "1" is available, then—and only then—may influencing factors with a measurability value of "0.5" be selected. However, these must then be explicitly designated as influencing factors that cannot be influenced directly (objectively).
Therefore, it is important that the Measurable Success Factors are chosen such that these are as close as possible to the Success Factors, i.e. the link should be as direct and strong as possible. More than one Success Factor and one Measurable Success Factor may be chosen.


## Structural Requirements

### 1. Element taxonomy (MANDATORY)

#### 1.1 Key Factor structure (updated: primary + optional secondary)
- **MANDATORY**: At least **ONE (1) Key Factor** per reference model.
- **MANDATORY**: Exactly **ONE (1) primary Key Factor** MUST be designated and tagged with [PRIMARY] at the beginning of `attributes.description`.
- **OPTIONAL**: Additional **secondary Key Factors** are allowed (e.g., multi-causal starting points or multiple intervention levers); tagged with [SECONDARY].
- **DEFINITION**: Key Factors represent central problem drivers and serve as primary intervention targets.
- **CLASSIFICATION**: `"element type": "Schlüsselfaktor"`
- **PRIMARY/SECONDARY TAGGING (schema-neutral)**:
  - **MANDATORY**: The primary Key Factor MUST include the tag `[PRIMARY]` at the beginning of `attributes.description`.
  - **OPTIONAL/RECOMMENDED**: Secondary Key Factors SHOULD include `[SECONDARY]` at the beginning of `attributes.description`.
- **REJECT**:
  - models with **zero** Key Factors
  - models with **more than one** `[PRIMARY]` Key Factor tag

#### 1.2 Success Factor structure (updated: not restricted to exactly one)
- **MANDATORY**: At least **ONE (1) Success Factor** per reference model.
- **ALLOWED**: Multiple Success Factors are permitted to represent multi-dimensional goal systems (e.g., quality, cost, time, sustainability).
- **DEFINITION**: Long-term, overarching objectives (e.g., product quality, customer satisfaction). These are the factors at ‘the top’ of the network, i.e., at the end of the cause–effect chains that provide the justification of the research.
- **CLASSIFICATION**: `"element type": "Erfolgsfaktor"`
- **MANDATORY (proxy rule)**: If a selected Success Factor has `measurability < 1.0`, the Success Factor remains unchanged and at least one measurable proxy MUST be added.
  - **CLASSIFICATION (proxy)**: `"element type": "Messbarer Erfolgsfaktor"`, measurability = 1.0. 
  - **DEFINITION**: The factors whose desired values are taken as Measurable Success Criteria are the Measurable Success Factors. Measurable Success Factor can be all influencing factors with measurability = 1.
    **MANDATORY**: The proxy selection priority is: (1) measurability = 1.0 on a direct path to the selected Success Factor; (2) if none exists, measurability = 0.5 with explicit justification and measurement method. Factors with measurability = 0 are not allowed as measurable proxies.

  - **TRACEABILITY (schema-neutral)**: The proxy relationship MUST be explained in the proxy's `attributes.description` (e.g., "Proxy for Erfolgsfaktor X because …").
- **REJECT**: models with **zero** Success Factors.

#### 1.3 Influencing Factors classification (updated: remove 3–7 recommendation)
- **MANDATORY**: All other factors MUST be classified as Influencing Factors.
- **CLASSIFICATION**: `"element type": "Einflussfaktoren"`
- **REMOVED**: No fixed recommendation of "3–7 Influencing Factors".
- **REVIEW REQUIRED**: Models with **>10** Influencing Factors (complexity and maintainability check).

### 2. Causal chain / network structure (MANDATORY)

#### 2.1 Causal network topology (updated to support network structure with feedback loops)
- **MANDATORY**: The **primary Key Factor** SHOULD be a start node (no incoming connections within the model), but secondary Key Factors MAY have incoming connections if justified.
- **MANDATORY**: All Success Factors SHOULD be end nodes (no outgoing connections within the model), but feedback loops to Influencing Factors are allowed if they represent dynamic system behavior.
- **MANDATORY**: All Influencing Factors MUST be causally positioned between Key Factor(s) and Success Factor(s).
- **STRUCTURE (network-based, dynamic)**:
  - Primary flow: `Key Factor(s) → [Influencing Factors] → [Measurable Success Factor(s)] → Success Factor(s)`
  - Allowed: 
    - Feedback loops and cyclic connections representing dynamic system behavior:
    - From Influencing Factors back to other Influencing Factors (reinforcement loops, time delays)
    - From Success Factors back to Influencing Factors (if representing dynamic outcomes feeding back into the system)
    - Between Key Factors and Influencing Factors (representing mutual influences or learning)
- **RATIONALE**: Positive and negative feedback loops are permitted and must be marked as such so that the "amplification" or "attenuation" of a mechanism is immediately and more easily recognizable.
- CRITICAL: All feedback loops MUST be explicitly justified in connection descriptions (mechanism, time horizon, feedback mechanism).
- **REJECT**:
  - Influencing Factors with no connection to any Key→Success path.
  - Feedback loops without explicit causal explanation.

#### 2.2 Path requirements (updated to support dynamic network topology with feedback)
- **MANDATORY**: There MUST be at least one directed path from the **primary Key Factor** to **at least one** Success Factor.
- **MANDATORY**: Each Key Factor (primary and secondary) MUST have at least one directed path to **at least one** Success Factor.
- **MANDATORY**: Maximum path length for primary chains: **4 directed connections** between any Key Factor and any Success Factor along a valid path.
- **ALLOWED (NEW)**: Feedback loops and cyclic connections representing dynamic system behavior:
  - From Influencing Factors back to other Influencing Factors (reinforcement loops, time delays)
  - From Success Factors back to Influencing Factors (if representing dynamic outcomes feeding back into the system)
  - Between Key Factors and Influencing Factors (representing mutual influences or learning)
- **CRITICAL**: All feedback loops MUST be explicitly justified in connection descriptions (mechanism, time horizon, feedback mechanism)
- **REJECT**:
  - Influencing Factors with no connection to any Key→Success path
  - Feedback loops without explicit causal explanation


### 3. Deterministic Derivation Protocol (NEW, MANDATORY)
#### 3.1 Step 1 - Problem normalization
#### 3.1.a Semantic matching specification (MANDATORY)
- To ensure reproducibility, semantic matching MUST use the following fixed setup:
  - Embedding model: `sentence-transformers/paraphrase-multilingual-mpnet-base-v2`
  - Similarity function: cosine similarity
  - Numeric precision for logs: 4 decimals
  - Equality tolerance for comparisons: 0.01

- Input text normalization (MANDATORY, in this exact order):
  1. convert to lowercase
  2. trim leading/trailing whitespace
  3. normalize German umlauts: ae/oe/ue/ss
  4. remove punctuation except hyphen
  5. collapse multiple spaces to a single space

- Semantic query construction:
  - Query text = normalized neutral scope statement from Step 1.
  - Candidate text per node = normalized "label + space + attributes.description".

- Semantic score per node:
  - `semantic_score(node) = cosine(embedding(query_text), embedding(candidate_text))`

- Minimum acceptance threshold:
  - A node is semantically admissible iff `semantic_score >= 0.5`.

- Deterministic ranking and ties:
  - Rank by semantic_score descending.
  - If `abs(score_i - score_j) <= 0.01`, apply tie-breakers in this order:
    1. higher out-degree in candidate subgraph
    2. higher influenceability
    3. alphabetical label

- Reproducibility log fields (MANDATORY):
  - model_name, similarity_function, threshold, tolerance,
  - normalized_query_text,
  - for each node: normalized_candidate_text, semantic_score

#### 3.2 Step 2 - Candidate extraction from Main Model
Candidate extraction MUST follow this fixed pipeline:

1. Build semantic candidate set `C_sem`:
   - include nodes with `semantic_score >= 0.5` according to section 3.1.a

2. Select base candidate set `C0`:
  - `C0 = C_sem`

3. Expand to `C1` by local topology:
   - add all nodes within directed distance 1 from any node in `C0`

4. Expand to `C2` by bridge paths:
   - add all nodes that lie on at least one directed path between two nodes in `C1`
   - only paths with length <= 3 are admissible

5. Remove isolated nodes:
   - remove nodes with no incident edge within `C2`

6. Final candidate set:
   - `C_final = C2 \ isolated_nodes`

7. Mandatory extraction log:
   - `|C_sem|`
  - semantic threshold and tolerance used (`threshold`, `tolerance`)
   - counts after each expansion step (`|C0|`, `|C1|`, `|C2|`),
   - list of removed isolated nodes with reason

#### 3.3 Step 3 - Key Factor scoring
For each potential node, compute score S_key:
- +3 if already Schluesselfaktor in Main Model
- +2 if out-degree > in-degree in candidate subgraph
- +2 if influenceability = 1
- +1 if influenceability = 0.5
- +1 if measurability = 1
- +0.5 if measurability = 0.5
- +1 if node is in a relevant causal loop (a directed cycle that contains at least one edge on a valid Primary-Key-to-Success path)
- -2 if primarily an outcome (out-degree = 0 within the candidate subgraph OR tagged/classified as Erfolgsfaktor/Messbarer Erfolgsfaktor)
Primary Key Factor = highest S_key.
Tie-breakers (strict order):
1) higher out-degree
2) lower in-degree
3) higher influenceability
4) alphabetical label

#### 3.4 Step 4 - Success Factor scoring
- Candidate set: downstream outcomes reachable from primary Key Factor within path length <= 4.
Score S_success:
- +3 if already Erfolgsfaktor or Messbarer Erfolgsfaktor
- +2 if downstream sink in candidate subgraph
- +2 if description denotes target-state quality/effectiveness/value/satisfaction/predictability
- -2 if primarily operational intermediate
Select highest score; keep max 2 unless user requests multi-dimensional target system.
Tie-breakers (strict order):
1. shortest distance to the primary key factor
2. highest betweenness on target paths
3. highest literature coverage of incoming edges
4. alphabetical

#### 3.5 Step 5 - Proxy rule
- If no Success Factor was found, the user must enter an anticipated “Success Criteria” and “Measurable Success Criteria” because “… criteria are needed to be able to judge the outcome of the research against the research goals.”
- If the user is unable to explicitly name one or both of these, or actively refuses to provide the input, then one is anticipated and derived based on the "observed problem." However, this must then be appropriately marked as a criterion "generated by AI".
- Proxy must have measurability = 1.0 and direct path to Success Factor.
- Following the proposal ("generated by AI "), the user must be given the option to either accept the criterion and proceed with the subsequent steps or to modify it. This initial proposal ("generated by AI ") provides the user with a general frame of reference and allows them to evaluate whether the anticipated goal has been correctly selected from their perspective.
Only after confirming or adjusting the criterion can either an RM based on the proposal (“generated by AI”) be finalized and a corresponding IM subsequently generated, or—following adjustment—the RM be iterated with the user until it is complete from their perspective, allowing the corresponding IM to be derived.
- Fixed State-Flow:
  - STATE A: User provides both criteria
  - STATE B: User provides one -> AI supplies the other
  - STATE C: User provides neither -> AI makes a suggestion + explicit confirmation required
  - Without confirmation: RM status = draft_only

#### 3.6 Step 6 - Influencing Factor selection
- Include factors on at least one valid path from primary Key Factor to Success Factor.
- Rank by path participation count and causal necessity.
- Keep smallest set preserving at least one complete path and all justified essential loops.



### 4. Source attribution (MANDATORY)

#### 4.1 Literature-based connections (priority 1)
- **TARGET**: >50% of all connections with literature sources `[1]`, `[2]`, etc.
- **MANDATORY**: Literature sources MUST be documented in `connection.attributes.description`.
- **FORMAT**: Full academic citation (APA style).
- **REJECT**: literature labels without corresponding documentation.

#### 4.2 Empirical evidence (priority 2)
- **ALLOWED**: stakeholder experience `[E]`, assumption `[A]`, or own investigations `[O]`.
- **MANDATORY**: Describe the evidence basis in `connection.attributes.description`.
- **LIMIT**: Max. 50% of connections without literature basis.

#### 4.3 Invalid sources
- **REJECT**: connections with `[?]` (unknown source)
- **REJECT**: connections without source attribution

### 5. Measurability and influenceability criteria

#### 5.1 Measurability (`measurability`)
**Scale: 0, 0.5, or 1.0 (discrete values, not continuous)**

- **0 = Not directly measurable** (z.B. qualitative Befragung, subjektive Beobachtung ohne klare Metrik)
  - No clear quantitative or defined qualitative indicators
  - Requires expert judgment for assessment
  - Example: "Vertrauen im Team", "Kulturelle Akzeptanz"

- **0.5 = Partially/indirectly measurable** (z.B. qualitative Daten, aber ergänzt durch quantitative Indikatoren oder Skalen)
  - Measurable through qualitative data with structured indicators or scales
  - Combination of qualitative assessment and quantitative proxies
  - Example: "Qualität der Kommunikation" (via surveys + frequency analysis)

- **1 = Directly measurable** (z.B. Kennzahlen, Anzahl, Zeitmessungen, Scores, klar definierte Metriken)
  - Clear numerical values, counts, time measurements, well-defined metrics
  - No ambiguity in measurement
  - Example: "Anzahl der gelösten Issues", "Durchschnittliche Cycle Time"

- **MANDATORY**: Key Factors MUST be influenceable and measurable. preferred: `influenceability = 1`; only if no other option is available, then `influenceability = 0.5`, `measurability ≥ 0.5`, Preference for `measurability = 1.0`. ("No other option" = all candidates with influenceability=1 violate at least one MUST criterion (e.g., path completeness, source rule, taxonomy).)
- **MANDATORY**: Measurable Success Factors MUST be highly measurable (`measurability = 1.0`).
- **MANDATORY**: Success Factors may have lower measurability (`measurability ≥ 0`), but at least one measurable proxy should exist.
- **HARD RULE**: new factors only with metrics (influenceability and measurability) – otherwise, no merge.
- **DOCUMENTATION**: Measurement methods MUST be specified in `attributes.description`.

#### 5.2 Influenceability (`influenceability`)
**Scale: 0, 0.5, or 1.0 (discrete values, not continuous)**

- **0 = Difficult or not directly influenceable** (schwer beeinflussbar)
  - External factors, boundary conditions, not under control
  - Require indirect mitigation strategies
  - Example: "Marktnachfrage", "Regulatorische Anforderungen"

- **0.5 = Partially influenceable via intermediary factors** (teilweise beeinflussbar / nur über mittlebare Faktoren)
  - Influenceable only through influencing other factors
  - Requires multi-step interventions
  - Example: "Teamvertrauen" (influenced via better communication, shared goals)

- **1.0 = Directly influenceable** (direkt beeinflussbar, z.B. konkrete Maßnahmen, Regeln, Tools)
  - Direct control through concrete measures, rules, tools, processes
  - Clear intervention points
  - Example: "Durchsatzzeit" (via process changes), "Dokumentation" (via tool implementation)

- **MANDATORY**: Key Factors MUST be influenceable and measurable. preferred: `influenceability = 1`; only if no other option is available, then `influenceability = 0.5`, `measurability ≥ 0.5`, Preference for `measurability = 1.0`.
- **EXPECTATION**: Success Factors typically have low influenceability (often ≤ 0.5), as they are outcomes.
- **LOGIC CHECK**: Influenceability MUST be consistent with factor role and design research philosophy (focus on actionable factors).

### 6. Reproducibility Metrics and Boundary Conditions (NEW)
#### 6.1 Required graph metrics
For each selected Key/Success/Influencing factor in RM derivation log, report:
- in-degree, out-degree, and degree delta (out - in) in candidate subgraph
- normalized closeness centrality
- normalized betweenness centrality
- shortest path length from primary Key Factor to Success Factor(s)

#### 6.2 Deterministic metric use
- Metrics are decision support and must follow fixed tie-break order (Section 3.3).
- If metric values differ by at most 0.01 (`abs(metric_i - metric_j) <= 0.01`), apply next tie-breaker.
- If still tied, apply alphabetical label.

#### 6.3 Path preservation constraint
- Minimal path preservation is mandatory: removing any retained influencing factor must not destroy all primary Key -> Success paths unless that factor is explicitly marked optional. At least one "primary key to success" path must remain.

#### 6.4 Reproducibility log (MANDATORY)
Each RM must provide a derivation log with:
- normalized problem statement and semantic matching configuration
- candidate list for Key Factors
- scoring table for Key Factor selection
- scoring table for Success Factor selection
- proxy creation or non-creation rationale
- excluded factors and exclusion reasons
Without this log, RM status is "not fully reproducible".

### 7. Connection semantics

#### 7.1 Direction and polarity
- **MANDATORY**: All connections MUST be directed (`"direction": "directed"`).
- **MANDATORY**: Connection type MUST use DRM polarities (`++`, `+-`, `-+`, `--`).
- **RECOMMENDATION**: Positive main chain from Key Factor(s) to Success Factor(s), where plausible.

#### 7.2 Connection descriptions
- **MANDATORY**: Each connection MUST explain the causal mechanism in `attributes.description`.
- **FORMAT**: "Factor X leads to Factor Y through mechanism Z."
- **LENGTH**: 20–150 words per description.

### 8. Validation steps

#### 8.1 Automated validation
1. JSON schema compliance (`lint_blueprint.py`)
2. Element/connection ID uniqueness
3. Reference integrity (`from`/`to` refer to existing IDs)
4. Factor taxonomy compliance
5. **Topology validation**:
   - exactly one `[PRIMARY]` Key Factor tag
   - Primary Key Factor SHOULD be a start node (or justified if not)
   - Success Factor(s) SHOULD be end nodes (or justified if not)
   - No isolated factors
   - Check: metrics_present_for_all_selected_nodes
   - Check: tie_breaker_trace_complete
6. **Feedback loop validation**: All feedback loops have explicit causal explanations

#### 8.2 Content validation
1. **Network topology** (dynamic, not strictly linear):
   - Primary Key Factor SHOULD have no incoming connections (justified if present)
   - Success Factor(s) SHOULD have no outgoing connections (justified if feedback loops exist)
   - Feedback loops are documented with explicit causal mechanisms
2. **Path completeness**:
   - at least one primary path: Primary Key → some Success Factor
   - each Key Factor reaches at least one Success Factor (direct or via feedback)
3. **Network integrity**:
   - all Influencing Factors are connected to at least one Key→Success path
   - feedback loops represent valid causal mechanisms (not circular reasoning)
4. Compute source attribution ratio
5. Validate measurability/influenceability consistency (using 0, 0.5, 1.0 scale)
6. Check factor formulation according to DRM principles ("attribute-of-an-element" phrasing)

#### 8.3 Quality metrics
- **Evidence Coverage**: ≥75% literature sources `[1-9]+`
- **Topological Integrity**: 100% - Primary Key Factor is a start node (or justified); all Success Factors are end nodes (or justified); no isolated factors
- **Path Completeness**: 100% - each Key Factor participates in a valid Key→Success chain (primary paths)
- **Network Dynamics**: Feedback loops (if present) are explicitly justified with causal mechanisms
- **Factor Integration**: 100% - all Influencing Factors are integrated into at least one Key→Success argument chain
- **Factor Precision**: 100% - all factors follow "attribute-of-an-element" formulation
- **Measurability Consistency**: Key Factors measurable (≥0.5, prefer 1.0), influenceability (1.0, only if no other option is available, then 0.5); Measurable Success Factors = 1.0; Success Factors ≥0.

## Summary (V5 - Updated with Dynamic Network Topology)

Reference Models represent the current state for specific problems. They:
1. Have **at least one Key Factor**, with **exactly one designated as the primary Key Factor** (preferably start node); **secondary Key Factors** are allowed.
2. Have **at least one Success Factor** (preferably end node); **multiple Success Factors** are allowed to reflect multi-dimensional objectives.
3. Use **Influencing Factors** and **feedback loops** to model complex, dynamic causal chains linking Key Factor(s) to Success Factor(s). All factors integrated into at least one valid Key→Success argument chain (no isolated factors).
4. **Support network topology** with feedback loops:
   - Primary flow: Key Factor(s) → Influencing Factors → Success Factor(s)
   - Allowed: Feedback from Success Factors to Influencing Factors, between Influencing Factors (reinforcement), and between Key Factors and Influencing Factors (dynamic mutual influences)
   - All feedback loops MUST be explicitly justified with causal mechanisms
5. Are grounded in **literature evidence** (>50% coverage) and transparent source attribution.
6. Use **discrete measurability/influenceability scales** (0, 0.5, 1.0) for consistency and clarity.
7. Follow **DRM attribute-of-element** formulation and document causal mechanisms for each connection.
8. Serve as the foundation for creating **Impact Models**.

Reference Models establish the baseline understanding of complex, dynamic systems necessary for designing and evaluating interventions.
